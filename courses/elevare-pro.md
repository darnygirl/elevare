## Stage 3 — Deliver (monthly rolling, 4-week themes)

### Week 1 — High-stakes meetings

**Learning objectives:**
- Lead a meeting without ceding control when pushback comes
- Interject politely when someone else is dominating
- Disagree strongly without damaging the relationship
- Recover from a mistake mid-meeting without losing credibility

**Core frameworks taught:**
- **PREP framework** for opening opinions: Point → Reason → Example → Point
- **Interjection phrases bank** (15 phrases) — "Can I jump in on that specific point?" / "Building on what Sarah said..." / "I want to push back on that respectfully — here's why"
- **Disagreement escalator** — 4 levels from soft to firm, with register-matched language at each
- **Recovery language** — "Let me rephrase that" / "What I meant was" / "Scratch that — the actual point is..."

**Session plans:**
- 1:1 (60 min): Role-play the student's specific upcoming meeting. Coach plays difficult counterpart. Score against framework usage.
- Small group (60 min, 3 students): Simulated cross-functional meeting. Each student plays a different function (engineering / product / sales). 20-min meeting + 20-min debrief per pair.

**Homework (between sessions):**
- 3× AI role-plays on student's weakest framework
- Watch 1 assigned TED talk for language patterns, submit 5 phrases noted
- 1 written reflection: describe your most recent difficult meeting using the frameworks

**Milestone:** by end of Week 1, student can deploy PREP + interjection + disagreement escalator in a practice meeting without notes.

---

### Week 2 — Presentations & pitches

**Learning objectives:**
- Structure a 3-min / 10-min / 30-min presentation using proven narrative frameworks
- Handle Q&A without defaulting to "great question" or defensive postures
- Tell stories in English that land — not just translate from L1
- Close a pitch with a specific ask, not vague next steps

**Core frameworks taught:**
- **MOTH framework** for storytelling: Moment → Obstacle → Transformation → Hook
- **SCQA for openings** — Situation → Complication → Question → Answer
- **Q&A bridge language** (10 phrases) — "The short answer is X. The longer answer is..." / "That's not something I have data on, but here's what I do know..."
- **Close-with-ask** — explicit, specific, measurable asks vs vague next steps

**Session plans:**
- 1:1 (60 min): Student brings a real upcoming presentation / pitch. Coach critiques structure + delivers frameworks. Student redelivers after coaching.
- Small group: 3 pitches × 5 min each, 10-min peer feedback per pitch, 5-min coach synthesis.

**Homework:**
- Record your pitch/presentation (phone camera) → transcribe → identify 3 specific language fixes
- 3 AI role-plays: Q&A handling on your topic with difficult audience types
- Study 1 assigned pitch deck / keynote, identify the frameworks in use

**Milestone:** by end of Week 2, student can deliver a 5-min pitch with MOTH + SCQA + explicit close without stumbling.

---

### Week 3 — Negotiation & difficult conversations

**Learning objectives:**
- Anchor pricing / terms at your target without apology
- Push back without sounding combative
- Deliver bad news (firing, declining, rejecting) with dignity for both parties
- Say "no" as a complete sentence when needed

**Core frameworks taught:**
- **BATNA positioning language** — how to signal alternatives without bluffing
- **Mirror + label** (from Chris Voss) adapted for English negotiation
- **Bad news sandwich** — context → message → path-forward
- **"No" as a complete sentence** — 5 register variations from peer-to-peer to report-to-boss
- **Silence as strategy** — when NOT to fill the pause

**Session plans:**
- 1:1 (60 min): Student's current or upcoming negotiation is the lesson. Coach plays the counterparty. Multiple takes.
- Small group: 3-way negotiation simulation (supplier, buyer, internal stakeholder). Students rotate roles.

**Homework:**
- Record 3 AI role-plays: price negotiation, delivering bad news to a team member, declining a client request
- Study 1 assigned negotiation transcript, identify the moves
- Apply one framework in a real conversation this week, report back

**Milestone:** by end of Week 3, student can deliver bad news in English with appropriate register and handle 3 rounds of pushback on a negotiation point.

---

### Week 4 — Written communication (email, Slack, professional writing)

**Learning objectives:**
- Match tone to context across email / Slack / Teams / LinkedIn / client-facing docs
- Write for scanning — busy executives read first sentence + bolded phrases
- Avoid over-politeness that undermines authority
- Write persuasively when stakes are high (proposal, reference, complaint)

**Core frameworks taught:**
- **Register matrix** — 6 contexts × tone markers
- **BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front)** for executive email
- **CC discipline** — when to cc, when not to, politics of the To:/Cc: line
- **Polite ≠ effective** — how over-softening loses credibility
- **Written negotiation** — the email that does the work of a 20-min call

**Session plans:**
- 1:1 (60 min): Student brings 3 real emails they've sent recently. Coach critiques + rewrites together.
- Small group: each student writes a difficult email (scenario provided), swap + peer review + coach synthesis.

**Homework:**
- Rewrite 5 of your own sent emails using the frameworks. Submit before/after.
- 1 Slack-register exercise: rewrite a Slack message 3 different ways (peer / manager / client-facing).
- Write one "difficult email" scenario we supply.

**Milestone:** by end of Week 4, student can produce written work that matches register, uses BLUF, and avoids over-politeness traps.

## Post-Week 4 — Rolling programme

The 4-week theme set repeats with new scenarios customized to student's current work. Month 2 themes pull from same 4 framework sets but use:

- Student's actual upcoming meetings / pitches / negotiations / emails as material
- Industry-specific variants (tech / finance / medical / real estate / consulting)
- Progression: frameworks move from explicit (conscious use) to implicit (automatic)

**Long-term track:** Pro students typically stay 4-8 months. Graduation is when they self-report: "I'm not thinking about English in meetings anymore — I'm thinking about the meeting."
