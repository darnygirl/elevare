## 1 · Why most OET prep misses the real bar

Most OET prep is built for Cambridge IELTS-style test takers — academic readers who want a certificate. It teaches you the paper format, gives you practice questions, and trusts you to self-correct.

That works if you're already at Grade B and want a validation course. It **doesn't work** if you're at Grade C+ because:

- **No one is listening to you speak.** OET speaking is a performance under time. Self-study can't fix a performance gap. You need a coach who hears you stumble, names the pattern, and drills you out of it.
- **The rubric is specific and unforgiving.** OET scores you on 5 dimensions: intelligibility, fluency, linguistic range, grammar/accuracy, and appropriateness of register. Most candidates fix 2-3 and leave the other 2-3 alone. Grade B requires all 5 at a consistent level.
- **Clinical plausibility matters.** OET role-plays are written by clinicians. Your responses need to be realistic — not just grammatically correct. If you've never been coached by someone who understands both the OET rubric AND clinical reality, you're guessing.

**The pattern:** Grade C candidates are usually 60-80% of the way to B on paper, but the last 20% requires a very specific kind of practice you can't do alone.

## 2 · The 7 gap-to-B mistakes (and the fixes)

### Mistake 1 — Rushing the opener

**What it looks like:** The first 30 seconds of OET speaking feel awkward. You greet the patient, introduce yourself, and rush straight into questions. The examiner marks you down on *appropriateness of register*.

**Why it costs you:** The opener sets the examiner's impression of your fluency + register for the whole 5 minutes. A rushed start gets graded against the rest of the paper even when the rest is good.

**The fix:**
- Settle first. Greet. Introduce yourself by role, not name only. "Hello Mr. Jones, I'm the nurse looking after you this morning — is it alright if I ask a few questions about what brought you in today?"
- Rehearse 5 opener variants until they're automatic. Practice them with someone who can score pace + tone.
- Under pressure, slow down deliberately. Slow is perceived as confident.

---

### Mistake 2 — History-taking without acknowledgement

**What it looks like:** You ask the patient "When did the pain start?" → they say "Three days ago, it was terrible" → you ask "How severe is the pain on a scale 1-10?" → skipping past their "terrible."

**Why it costs you:** *Appropriateness of register* and *linguistic range* get marked down for missed acknowledgements. The examiner sees you as running a checklist rather than managing a patient.

**The fix:**
- Between every patient answer and your next question, insert an acknowledgement: "That sounds difficult — I'm sorry you've been dealing with that."
- Build a bank of 10-15 acknowledgement phrases, rotate them. Repetition ("I understand, I understand") gets marked down for *linguistic range*.
- Drill: take any 5-question history and add an acknowledgement between each question. Record yourself.

---

### Mistake 3 — Jargon creeping into patient-facing language

**What it looks like:** You explain a procedure to the patient and slip into clinical shorthand: "We'll do an ECG and then probably a troponin if your history's significant."

**Why it costs you:** *Intelligibility* and *appropriateness* both drop. OET examiners aren't patients, but they score you against how a non-medical patient would experience your explanation.

**The fix:**
- For every clinical term, rehearse the plain-English swap: "ECG" → "a tracing of your heart rhythm" / "troponin" → "a blood test that checks for damage to your heart muscle".
- Build a 50-term swap list for the specialties most common in your exam. Practice until swaps are reflexive.
- Test yourself: could your 10-year-old niece understand what you just said? If no, rephrase.

---

### Mistake 4 — Grammar slippage under pressure

**What it looks like:** You have good written grammar, but under the time pressure of speaking, articles drop, third-person -s disappears, and past tense slips into present. "He have pain since Tuesday" instead of "He has had pain since Tuesday."

**Why it costs you:** *Grammar/accuracy* is the single most reliable bandwidth-killer at Grade C. It's also the hardest to fix alone because you don't hear yourself doing it.

**The fix:**
- Record every practice session. Transcribe 1-2 per week. Circle every grammar slip. Look for patterns — you probably have 3-4 repeat patterns, not 30.
- Target those patterns with drills. Example: if third-person -s drops, practice 20 patient descriptions in present simple until it's automatic.
- A coach catches this 10x faster than self-study because they're listening for patterns you can't hear in yourself.

---

### Mistake 5 — Not using the rubric dimensions deliberately

**What it looks like:** You practice the paper. You do role-plays. But you don't know, after each practice, which of the 5 rubric dimensions you improved and which stayed the same.

**Why it costs you:** Practice without measurement = practice without progress. You do 20 role-plays and plateau at C+ because you're reinforcing the same patterns every time.

**The fix:**
- After every practice role-play, score yourself on the 5 dimensions: intelligibility / fluency / linguistic range / grammar/accuracy / appropriateness of register. Rate each 0-10.
- Identify the 2 lowest. Next session, design drills that target ONLY those 2.
- After 5 sessions of targeted work, re-score. You should see movement. If not, your drills aren't targeted correctly — a coach can diagnose this in 10 minutes.

---

### Mistake 6 — Unfamiliar exam-style patients

**What it looks like:** You practice history-taking with colleagues, friends, family, a tutor. They all play the role at a pace and fluency *you're comfortable with*. OET role-play patients aren't comfortable — they interrupt, misunderstand, repeat themselves, get emotional, say confusing things.

**Why it costs you:** On exam day, the unfamiliar patient behaviour throws you. Your real-world clinical English doesn't transfer because it's developed in contexts where patients adapt to you.

**The fix:**
- Practice with someone who deliberately plays unfamiliar patient variants: a confused elderly patient, an anxious first-time parent, a patient who speaks over you, a patient who doesn't understand your first explanation.
- Build tolerance for awkward silence. The examiner is listening to how you handle when the patient doesn't cooperate. Stumbling is fine. Freezing is not.
- A coach trained on OET will role-play these variants realistically. A well-meaning friend won't.

---

### Mistake 7 — Practicing speaking and writing, ignoring listening and reading

**What it looks like:** You focus 80% of your prep on speaking and writing because they feel hardest. Listening and reading are "the easy papers." You score C on reading/listening and B on speaking — but your overall grade fails because OET requires Grade B on ALL 4 papers.

**Why it costs you:** Many candidates retake because they passed speaking but failed reading. The "easy" papers get their own systematic prep time.

**The fix:**
- Allocate equal weekly time to all 4 papers. Not more, not less.
- Reading: practice under exam time limits. The pressure is what makes it hard, not the content.
- Listening: work with OET's specific accent range (UK/AU/NZ/IE). Practice on 2x speed once comfortable at 1x — exam day is lower pressure after.

## 3 · Self-study schedule if you have 12 weeks

**Weeks 1-3 — Diagnostic + foundations**
- Sit a full OET mock under real time. Score using the official rubric. Note your weakest paper + weakest rubric dimensions within speaking.
- Fix Mistake 1 + 2 (opener + acknowledgements) — focused drills, record daily.
- Listening + reading: 30 min daily.

**Weeks 4-6 — History-taking fluency**
- 10 role-plays across specialties. Record each. Score against rubric.
- Fix Mistake 3 (jargon swap) — build your 50-term list.
- Writing: 2 referral letters per week under time.

**Weeks 7-9 — Grammar + variant patients**
- Transcribe 2 role-plays/week. Identify your grammar patterns. Target drills.
- Practice with variant patient types (Mistake 6). Find a coach or peer for this.
- Second OET mock at Week 9.

**Weeks 10-12 — Integration + strategy**
- Weekly full mock under time. Score yourself against the rubric.
- Weak-spot pass — whichever rubric dimension is lowest gets targeted drilling.
- Exam-week protocol: routine, nutrition, sleep, first-30-seconds rehearsal.

## 4 · What if your exam is < 8 weeks away

If your exam is soon, inverse the priorities:

- **Days 1-14:** triage — score yourself against the rubric, identify your 3 weakest areas, commit 80% of time to those three.
- **Days 15-30:** daily mock speaking + rubric-scored writing. No new content, only practice.
- **Days 31-45:** integration — full mocks every 2 days under time. Weak-spot pass daily.
- **Days 46-56:** taper. Light practice only. Sleep. Exam protocol rehearsal.

An 8-week accelerator with a coach is dramatically more efficient than self-study for this timeline. Consider enrolling.

## 5 · How to tell if you're ready

You're ready for exam-day when:
- You've sat 3 full mocks under real time, all scoring Grade B on all 4 papers
- Your speaking role-plays feel automatic in openers, history-take, explaining, closing
- Your grammar patterns in speaking are stable — no slips under pressure
- You've experienced all the awkward patient variants without freezing

You're **not** ready when:
- You haven't done a full timed mock in the last 3 weeks
- One rubric dimension is still at C level
- You've been "studying" but haven't been scored by anyone in the last 2 weeks
- You're hoping rather than confident

If you're in the second category — get scored by someone external before you sit the exam. The £300+ exam fee + visa timeline cost dwarfs any coaching investment.
