# OET Diagnostic Rubric — Elevare Med Intake Assessment

> Score every new Elevare Med lead against this rubric during the free 10-min assessment call. Gives an honest baseline + tier-match + personalized 3-week plan. Conforms to the 5-stage methodology in `implementation-methodology.md` — this is the Stage-1 Intake tool.

---

## How to use this rubric

- Run during the assessment call — ~10 min including a 3-min role-play
- Score as you go (do NOT show student the scores until the end)
- Aim for honest scoring; a high-but-wrong score creates a refund later
- End of call: send the raw scores back with a 3-week starter plan within 24 hrs
- Keep signed copies in each student's record (Google Sheet → Airtable)

**Decision tree at the bottom tells you:** accept to Elevare Med / deflect to Speak / refer out.

## The 5 scored dimensions

OET scores on 5 dimensions per paper. For the diagnostic, we focus on **speaking** (the biggest differentiator at Grade C+) plus a brief listening + writing check. Reading is assessed via a short async exercise post-call.

### Dimension 1 — Intelligibility

> How easy it is to understand the student, at normal conversational pace, without asking them to repeat.

| Score | Indicator |
|---|---|
| 0-100 | Hard to follow; multiple repeat requests |
| 100-200 | Basic intelligibility but strain on listener |
| 200-300 | Fully intelligible most of the time; occasional strain |
| 300-400 | Clear, consistent intelligibility |
| 400-500 | Native-speaker-like clarity, zero strain |

**What to listen for:**
- Specific phonemes that disappear (common: /v/ vs /w/, /θ/ vs /t/ or /s/, final consonants dropped)
- Word stress placement (wrong stress can make otherwise correct words unintelligible)
- Pace — too fast, unclear; too slow, signals low confidence

**OET Grade B floor:** 350

---

### Dimension 2 — Fluency

> Smoothness of speech, absence of distracting hesitation, ability to sustain under pressure.

| Score | Indicator |
|---|---|
| 0-100 | Heavy hesitation, many long pauses, fillers dominate |
| 100-200 | Speech in short bursts; frequent restarts |
| 200-300 | Moderate flow with noticeable hesitation on complex content |
| 300-400 | Generally smooth; hesitation only on unfamiliar clinical scenarios |
| 400-500 | Near-native flow under pressure |

**What to listen for:**
- Filler density (um, uh, like) — count during the 3-min role-play
- Self-corrections — 1-2 is fine, 5+ is a red flag
- Pause length — natural pauses vs stuck-searching pauses

**OET Grade B floor:** 350

---

### Dimension 3 — Linguistic range

> Breadth of vocabulary, grammatical structures, and register the student can deploy.

| Score | Indicator |
|---|---|
| 0-100 | Basic, repetitive, limited to present tense + common words |
| 100-200 | Can describe but not compare, evaluate, or hypothesize |
| 200-300 | Deploys past/future, some comparative structures |
| 300-400 | Full tense range + modal verbs + register shifts |
| 400-500 | Idiomatic, nuanced, register-flexible |

**What to listen for:**
- Variety of vocabulary for same concept (did student say "pain" 15 times, or use "discomfort / ache / soreness / tenderness")?
- Grammar variety — did they use past perfect? Conditional? Passive?
- Register shifts — can they move from clinical precision to empathetic patient-facing in the same turn?

**OET Grade B floor:** 350

---

### Dimension 4 — Grammar &amp; accuracy

> Correct use of grammar structures under speaking pressure — NOT written grammar, which is often much higher.

| Score | Indicator |
|---|---|
| 0-100 | Many errors, some affecting meaning |
| 100-200 | Frequent errors on basic structures (articles, third-person -s, past tense irregulars) |
| 200-300 | Accurate on basic structures; errors on complex (conditionals, modals, passive) |
| 300-400 | Minor errors that don't affect meaning |
| 400-500 | Near-native accuracy |

**What to listen for (the classic Grade-C patterns):**
- Article drops ("patient has pain" instead of "the patient has pain")
- Third-person -s drops ("He have pain" instead of "He has pain")
- Present simple creeping into past-tense narratives
- Preposition mismatches (depend of vs depend on)
- Modal verb misuse (must vs have to vs should — these matter clinically)

**OET Grade B floor:** 350

---

### Dimension 5 — Appropriateness of register

> How well the student matches their language to the context — empathetic with patient, concise with team, formal in writing.

| Score | Indicator |
|---|---|
| 0-100 | One register only; same tone regardless of context |
| 100-200 | Can attempt register shift but executes inconsistently |
| 200-300 | Generally appropriate; occasional mismatches |
| 300-400 | Clear and consistent register-matching in clinical scenarios |
| 400-500 | Sophisticated — adjusts mid-turn to patient cues |

**What to listen for:**
- Jargon usage with patient (drops score) vs with team (appropriate)
- Acknowledgement before clinical questions ("That sounds difficult — when did it start?")
- Recovery from patient distress cues (empathy signals)
- Avoidance of over-polite softening that undermines authority

**OET Grade B floor:** 340 (this one is slightly more forgiving at the B border)

## The 3-min role-play script

Use one of these scenarios during the intake call. Pick based on student's stated specialty if possible.

### Scenario A — Chest pain history (general)
> "You're the nurse in A&amp;E. I'm a 58-year-old man who walked in 20 minutes ago. I've been having chest pain on and off for three days, and it's worse today. Please take a history from me. Start now."

**Interviewer notes:**
- Play lightly confused + anxious
- Give information when asked but don't volunteer
- If student asks "How severe is the pain?" — answer "It's bad. It's really bad."
- At ~2 min, inject a difficulty: "Can you explain what you're going to do? I'm scared."

**What to score:** opener (0-30 sec), history-taking structure, acknowledgement moments, jargon handling when asked to explain, closing.

### Scenario B — Post-operative patient handover (shift change)
> "You've just finished your shift. Please give me a handover on this patient: Mrs. Patel, 72, day 3 post-abdominal hysterectomy. She's had some pain management issues overnight and a low-grade fever starting 4 hours ago. Ready? Start when you are."

**Interviewer notes:**
- Don't ask clarifying questions for the first minute
- At minute 2, ask "What's her current pain score?" even if already covered (tests grace under re-covering old ground)
- Score: SBAR structure, completeness, confidence

### Scenario C — Explaining a procedure to an anxious patient
> "You're the nurse preparing me for an ECG. I don't know what it is. I'm worried it'll hurt. Explain the procedure and reassure me. Start now."

**Interviewer notes:**
- Interrupt twice with a worried question
- Use "I don't understand" at minute 1 if student uses jargon
- Score: jargon swap, empathy, clarity

## Listening mini-check (2 min)

Play this short audio clip (via Zoom screen share or a prepared recording):

> [2-min clip of a realistic SBAR handover between two nurses, accented as UK/AU/NZ/Irish depending on student's target country. If no recording available, read it aloud at natural pace with natural disfluencies.]

Then ask student to:
1. Summarize what just happened in 30 seconds
2. Answer: "What was the main concern being flagged?"

**What you're scoring:** listening at native pace, retention, comprehension of clinical context, ability to re-summarize.

**Grade B indicator:** can identify the main concern and 2 of 3 secondary details.

---

## Writing mini-check (async, post-call)

Email this prompt within 1 hr of the intake call:

> Write 80-120 words describing a routine patient scenario in your specialty. Any patient you've recently cared for. Describe what was happening, what you did, and what the outcome was. Submit within 24 hrs.

**What you're scoring:**
- Grammar accuracy
- Clinical precision in writing (often higher than speaking)
- Register (should be clinical/professional tone, not spoken-style)
- Vocabulary range

**Grade B indicator:** 80+ words, clinical register, 0-2 grammar errors affecting meaning.

## Tier-match decision tree

### ACCEPT to Elevare Med if:
- At least 3 of 5 speaking dimensions scoring 300+
- Listening 300+
- Writing 300+
- Student has 10+ weeks to exam date
- Student can commit to 30-min daily + 1 hr weekly

### DEFLECT to Elevare Speak ($199/mo) if:
- Speaking dimensions averaging under 300
- Writing strong but speaking weak at basic-structure level
- Student needs general fluency before specialty focus
- Recommend Speak for 2-3 months, then re-assess for Med

### REFER OUT (gentle deflection) if:
- Listening under 200 (struggles to follow native-pace English)
- Writing under 200 (fundamental grammar not in place)
- Suggest general English apps + return in 3-6 months

### OFFER 8-WEEK ACCELERATOR if:
- Student exam date is < 8 weeks away
- Speaking already at 250+ across dimensions
- Accelerator = compressed curriculum + 2× weekly coach + intensive mocks
- Priced same as 12-week programme ($699/mo)

## Coach training for this rubric

Any Elevare Med coach must:
1. Score 10 pre-recorded sample role-plays before taking a real intake call
2. Reach 85%+ agreement with senior coach on dimension scoring
3. Do their first 3 real intake calls with a senior coach listening silently

Drift check: every quarter, senior coach re-scores 5 random recent intakes. If any coach's scores diverge >50 points from senior benchmark consistently, re-training required.
